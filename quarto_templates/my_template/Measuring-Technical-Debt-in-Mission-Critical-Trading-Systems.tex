\chapter{Introduction}\label{introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}\label{background-and-motivation}

The increasing complexity of mission-critical trading systems has
introduced significant challenges in maintaining software quality over
time. Technical debt --- the implied cost of rework caused by choosing
expedient solutions over more robust approaches --- has become a
critical concern in financial technology environments where system
reliability is paramount.

\section{Problem Statement}\label{problem-statement}

Despite widespread acknowledgement of technical debt as a key risk
factor in enterprise IT, there remains a lack of standardised,
quantitative frameworks for measuring and managing it specifically
within mission-critical trading infrastructures. Current approaches are
largely qualitative and inconsistent, making it difficult to prioritise
remediation efforts effectively.

\section{Research Aims and
Objectives}\label{research-aims-and-objectives}

This thesis aims to develop a service-level quantitative framework for
measuring technical debt in mission-critical trading systems. The
specific objectives are:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  To review existing literature on technical debt measurement frameworks
  and service management practices
\item
  To identify and validate a set of quantifiable technical debt
  indicators relevant to trading systems
\item
  To design and apply a multi-criteria decision-making model (TOPSIS)
  for scoring and prioritising technical debt
\item
  To evaluate the framework against real-world data from a
  mission-critical trading environment
\item
  To assess the practical utility of the framework for IT managers and
  decision-makers
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Questions}\label{research-questions}

The study is guided by the following primary research question:

\begin{quote}
\emph{How can technical debt in mission-critical trading systems be
measured quantitatively at the service level?}
\end{quote}

Secondary questions include:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Which indicators most reliably capture technical debt in trading
  infrastructure?
\item
  How can TOPSIS be applied to rank services by accumulated technical
  debt?
\item
  What thresholds distinguish acceptable from critical debt levels?
\end{itemize}

\section{Significance of the Study}\label{significance-of-the-study}

This research contributes to both academic knowledge and industry
practice by providing a reproducible, data-driven methodology that
bridges IT service management and software quality engineering.

\chapter{Literature Review}\label{literature-review}

\section{Context of the Topic}\label{context-of-the-topic}

Technical debt has become one of the most consequential and least
consistently managed challenges in enterprise IT governance (Avgeriou
\emph{et al.}, 2024). Originally coined as a metaphor for the
compounding cost of releasing code that is not quite right, the concept
has since expanded far beyond its source code origins to encompass
architectural, infrastructure, process, and organisational dimensions of
complex IT systems (Kruchten, Nord and Ozkaya, 2012). Despite this
broadened scope, the dominant measurement ecosystem remains anchored at
the code level, relying on static analysis tools that are structurally
unable to measure the forms of debt most consequential in regulated
enterprise environments: unsupported infrastructure, unresolved security
vulnerabilities, degraded operational reliability, and the accumulated
consequences of vendor platform neglect. This structural mismatch is the
problem this review addresses.

The review is motivated by a specific gap at the intersection of three
fields: technical debt management, multi-criteria decision-making, and
mission-critical enterprise IT operations. No existing framework
provides a quantitative, service-level mechanism for measuring and
prioritising technical debt in environments such as trading IT, where
the most consequential debt is observable not in code repositories but
in operational outcomes including incident frequency, recovery time,
patch lag, and change failure rate (Ramasubbu and Kemerer, 2021). This
gap carries ethical as well as methodological dimensions. Any framework
that assigns risk scores to IT services in a financial institution
creates attribution effects: high-scoring services become organisational
liabilities, with implications for team resourcing, investment
decisions, and individual accountability that extend well beyond the
technical. These ethical dimensions are addressed explicitly within each
thematic section rather than deferred to a separate discussion.

\section{Perspective and Framework}\label{perspective-and-framework}

This review adopts a critical, problem-oriented perspective structured
to build a coherent argument: that existing measurement and
prioritisation approaches are inadequate for service-level enterprise IT
contexts, and that a TOPSIS-based multi-criteria framework applied to
service management data represents a defensible and novel contribution.
The review is organised into four thematic areas corresponding to the
four pillars of the research design: (1) the conceptual foundations of
technical debt; (2) existing measurement and prioritisation frameworks
and their limitations; (3) technical debt in financial and regulated
enterprise IT; and (4) multi-criteria decision-making and TOPSIS.

\section{Analytical Approach}\label{analytical-approach}

This review employs narrative synthesis rather than a formal systematic
review protocol, justified on two grounds. First, the research spans
three disciplines with heterogeneous methods and terminologies not
amenable to unified meta-analysis. Second, the objective is to construct
a theoretical and empirical argument in support of a novel design
science artefact (Hevner \emph{et al.}, 2004), for which narrative
synthesis is the methodologically appropriate instrument. Each thematic
section applies three analytical moves: synthesis, which identifies
convergent findings; critique, which surfaces limitations,
contradictions, and ethical tensions; and positioning, which traces
identified gaps directly to design decisions of the proposed framework.

\section{Conceptual Foundations of Technical
Debt}\label{conceptual-foundations-of-technical-debt}

\subsection{Origins and the Metaphor}\label{origins-and-the-metaphor}

The technical debt metaphor was introduced by Ward Cunningham in 1993,
characterising the release of not-quite-right code as analogous to
financial debt: short-term velocity is gained, but every subsequent
maintenance minute spent on that code constitutes interest, and if the
debt is not repaid it accumulates until the system becomes
unmaintainable (Haki \emph{et al.}, 2023). Kruchten, Nord and Ozkaya
(2012) gave the metaphor its most influential formal treatment,
proposing a two-dimensional model separating the deliberateness of debt
(deliberate versus inadvertent) from its visibility (visible versus
invisible), and populating a typology that includes code, design,
architectural, test, build, and documentation debt. This taxonomy
established that technical debt is not synonymous with poor coding
practice but is a governance phenomenon encompassing intentional
trade-offs under resource constraints as well as unrecognised systemic
degradation.

\subsection{Theoretical Development}\label{theoretical-development}

Subsequent empirical work confirmed and extended this framing. Besker
(2020) demonstrated across multiple industrial studies that software
developers waste approximately 23\% of their working time due to the
effects of existing technical debt, and that architectural debt exerts
the greatest negative influence on development productivity across all
debt sub-types. Bedi and Kaur (2022) showed through regression analysis
of open-source repositories that commit frequency, lines of code, code
smells, and test coverage are statistically significant predictors of
debt accumulation, while developer reputation metrics have no
significant effect, locating technical debt firmly in development
practices rather than developer quality. Ahmad \emph{et al.} (2026),
drawing on qualitative interviews with 24 experts across four
international companies, identified five non-technical dimensions of
debt accumulation in large-scale agile environments --- social dynamics,
process, people, documentation, and requirements --- with lack of
communication and collaboration as the primary drivers.

A more fundamental theoretical challenge comes from Vidoni, Codabux and
Fard (2022), who leverage game theory to argue that technical debt in
complex systems cannot be entirely removed and that its effects cannot
be considered managed even when individual items are repaid. This
concept of infinite technical debt challenges the implicit assumption
underlying all repayment-focused frameworks that debt is always
recoverable given sufficient resources. From an ethical standpoint, this
is significant: if a system has crossed into infinite debt territory,
prioritisation frameworks may create the appearance of governance
without substantive improvement, potentially providing false assurance
to regulators and senior management while deferring an inevitable
decommissioning decision.

\subsection{Expanding the Conceptual
Perimeter}\label{expanding-the-conceptual-perimeter}

The conceptual boundaries of technical debt now extend to every layer of
the enterprise IT stack. Kleinwaks, Batchelor and Bradley (2023)
conducted a systematic literature review of technical debt in systems
engineering and found that requirements, architecture, integration, and
verification artefacts all accumulate debt, and that software-oriented
management approaches are poorly adapted to multi-discipline engineering
lifecycles. Hanssen, Brataas and Martini (2019) identified scalability
debt as a distinct sub-type triggered by regulatory change,
demonstrating in an open banking context that sound architectural
decisions made before the PSD2 directive became sources of debt when new
scalability requirements were imposed without architectural revision.
Yang \emph{et al.} (2019) proposed a taxonomy of COTS-related technical
debt covering seven key types observable during systems acquisition,
establishing that vendor platform obsolescence is a form of technical
debt requiring management tools fundamentally different from those
applied to internally developed code. This expansion is directly
relevant to the present research: the most consequential debt in
financial trading IT is platform debt, infrastructure debt, and
vulnerability debt, none of which is reachable by standard static
analysis tooling.

\section{Measurement and Prioritisation
Frameworks}\label{measurement-and-prioritisation-frameworks}

\subsection{The Dominance of Static Analysis and Its
Limits}\label{the-dominance-of-static-analysis-and-its-limits}

The measurement of technical debt in practice is dominated by static
code analysis tools including SonarQube, SQALE, and CAST, each
operationalising debt as a weighted sum of estimated remediation effort
for rule violations detected by automated scanners (Biazotto \emph{et
al.}, 2024). Biazotto \emph{et al.} (2024), in a systematic mapping
study of 178 primary studies, identified 121 automation artefacts for
technical debt management, found that the majority operate at the code
level, that most require substantial human intervention even at higher
automation levels, and that integration across the full technical debt
management lifecycle remains unsolved. Ampatzoglou \emph{et al.} (2022)
present SDK4ED as the most comprehensive industry-oriented integration
of code-level measurement and prevention, reporting a three-year
evaluation confirming financial value to software organisations, while
simultaneously confirming that the platform provides limited support for
infrastructure-level or vendor platform debt. The ethical implication of
this limitation is non-trivial: tools that measure only what they can
reach create systematic blind spots, and organisations relying
exclusively on tool-generated estimates may allocate remediation
resources to low-risk cosmetic violations while high-risk unsupported
infrastructure accumulates unobserved.

Amanatidis \emph{et al.} (2020) evaluated the agreement among leading
technical debt assessment tools across 50 open-source projects and found
substantial inter-tool divergence: different tools examining the same
codebase produce materially different debt estimates and identify
different violation sets, with no single tool consistently dominant. A
debt estimate produced by a single tool is therefore not a neutral
measurement but a function of that tool's particular ruleset and
threshold calibrations. Practitioners who accept tool outputs as
objective assessments effectively delegate their prioritisation
judgement to an opaque algorithmic configuration. Albuquerque \emph{et
al.} (2023), in a systematic mapping study of 150 primary studies on
intelligent techniques applied to technical debt management, found that
most approaches operate at automation levels one and two, that
substantial human intervention remains necessary, and that
infrastructure debt and process debt remain chronically understudied
relative to code and architectural debt types.

\subsection{The Prioritisation Gap}\label{the-prioritisation-gap}

Lenarduzzi \emph{et al.} (2021), in the most comprehensive systematic
review of prioritisation strategies to date covering 44 primary studies,
found that existing approaches cluster around cost-based, value-based,
and risk-based strategies; that the vast majority propose a single
prioritisation dimension; that quantitative methods are far less common
than qualitative and heuristic approaches; and that empirical validation
on industrial datasets is rare. The review explicitly concludes that
technical debt prioritisation research is preliminary, that there is no
consensus on what the important factors are or how to measure them, and
that current research cannot be considered conclusive (Lenarduzzi
\emph{et al.}, 2021). This is the most direct empirical justification in
the literature for the design contribution pursued in this dissertation.

The divergence between tool-generated priorities and practitioner
behaviour is well documented. Alfayez \emph{et al.} (2023) conducted a
study with 89 participants across four companies, finding that the
overwhelming majority factored in both value and cost when making
repayment decisions rather than following any single strategy, that the
value of a debt item was consistently described as subjective and
context-dependent, and that no single approach addressed all developer
objectives. Wiese and Borowa (2023) reinforced this from a managerial
perspective, finding across 16 semi-structured interviews that nearly
none of the IT managers studied had established a formal technical debt
management process and that communicating about technical debt across
technical and business stakeholders was the primary identified
challenge. (\textbf{stochelBusinessDrivenTechnical2023?}) propose the
Continuous Debt Valuation Approach, which computes a financial value for
each debt item as a function of remediation cost, interest rate, and
business risk exposure, demonstrating through case studies that this
valuation significantly shifts prioritisation decisions relative to
effort-only approaches, though CoDVA remains anchored in
development-level data and does not incorporate service management
signals.

The ethical dimension of prioritisation deserves explicit
acknowledgement. Any framework assigning a priority score to a technical
debt item implicitly encodes value judgements about which systems and
which risks matter most. In a financial institution, a high priority
score attached to a trading platform component creates pressure on the
responsible team, influences investment decisions, and may be used in
post-incident accountability processes. A framework that does not
disclose and justify its weighting logic creates the conditions for
algorithmic accountability gaps, where consequential decisions appear
technically derived rather than normatively chosen.

\section{Technical Debt in Financial and Regulated Enterprise
IT}\label{technical-debt-in-financial-and-regulated-enterprise-it}

\subsection{The Financial Services
Context}\label{the-financial-services-context}

Financial services organisations are characterised by decades of layered
legacy systems, high regulatory compliance requirements, strict service
availability obligations, and architectural constraints imposed by
vendor platforms that cannot be freely refactored (Haki \emph{et al.},
2023). Haki \emph{et al.} (2023) conducted clinical research at Credit
Suisse, a global financial services company headquartered in Zurich,
designing and evaluating a digital nudge for technical debt management
across more than 3,000 IT applications. The Credit Suisse technical debt
taxonomy distinguished four operationally significant types: code
quality debt, infrastructure lifecycle debt (cumulative end-of-life
months of unsupported components), vulnerability debt (weighted
vulnerability counts from network scanners), and automation debt (build
and deployment maturity). This taxonomy independently confirms that a
major financial institution prioritises infrastructure and vulnerability
debt over code debt as the most strategically significant liabilities at
enterprise scale.

The study's results must however be interpreted with care. The
quantitative evaluation involved three software development teams, of
which two reduced their debt index within a year while one did not, with
management intent identified as the decisive variable rather than nudge
design quality (Haki \emph{et al.}, 2023). This is a thin evidential
basis for generalisation across a global financial IT landscape. The
clinical research design, which embedded the researchers as both
designers and evaluators, also introduces positionality concerns that
limit independence of the evaluation. These limitations do not
invalidate the conceptual contribution, but they counsel against
treating the study as strong causal evidence for any particular
technical debt management mechanism.

\subsection{Enterprise Systems and the Outsourcing
Context}\label{enterprise-systems-and-the-outsourcing-context}

(\textbf{ramasubbuTechnicalDebtReliability2015?}) established through
longitudinal analysis of a commercial enterprise system deployed at 48
client firms that technical debt decreases system reliability
non-linearly: modular maintenance targeted at debt reduction was
approximately 53\% more effective than architectural maintenance in
reducing client error-driven failures, but simultaneously generated an
83\% higher probability of vendor error-driven failures. This
non-linearity is critical for any prioritisation framework:
interventions that reduce one dimension of operational risk may
simultaneously increase another, and a framework that treats criteria
independently without modelling their interactions risks recommending
actions that are locally optimal but globally harmful. Ramasubbu and
Kemerer (2021) extended this with 1,824 outsourced remediation projects
involving 424 client organisations, finding that control balancing
improved remediation outcomes only when a long-term migration roadmap
had been identified; without such a roadmap, intensified coordination
actually reduced remediation performance. This implies that
prioritisation frameworks must be coupled to strategic roadmaps, not
deployed as standalone ranking tools.

\subsection{DORA Metrics as Operational Debt
Proxies}\label{dora-metrics-as-operational-debt-proxies}

Forsgren, Humble and Kim (2018) established the DORA framework across
more than 2,000 organisations, demonstrating that deployment frequency,
lead time for changes, mean time to restore service, and change failure
rate distinguish high-performing delivery organisations from
low-performing ones. The critical property of the DORA metrics for the
present research is that they are derived entirely from operational
service management data rather than code repositories. Forsgren, Humble
and Kim (2018) demonstrate that accumulated debt in architecture,
infrastructure, and process manifests in degraded operational throughput
before it becomes visible in code, positioning service management data
as a legitimate and sufficient measurement substrate when source code is
structurally inaccessible. Neil, Häger and Andersen (2009) complement
this with a hybrid dynamic Bayesian network model validated against
banking operational loss data, demonstrating that incident frequency and
severity in financial IT follow identifiable probabilistic patterns
usable for risk prioritisation without complete knowledge of the
underlying system state.

The ethical implication of using incident data as a debt proxy requires
explicit attention. Incident records are generated and owned by
operations teams without the explicit consent of the development teams
whose technical decisions contributed to those incidents. Using this
data as a ranking input creates a governance artefact with
accountability implications, potentially attributing operational
failures to specific teams in ways affecting performance evaluations and
budget allocations. Any framework deploying this approach must establish
clear data governance principles and disclose how incident data is
attributed, aggregated, and used in its outputs.

\subsection{Security Debt and Patch
Management}\label{security-debt-and-patch-management}

Feutrill \emph{et al.} (2018) established that critical vulnerabilities
with network attack vectors are exploited within a median of
approximately 30 days of publication, creating a direct empirical link
between patch currency and operational security risk. Markkanen and
Frantti (2023) demonstrated through simulation that individualised,
risk-adjusted patch timelines reduce both security exposure and
operational disruption relative to uniform patching policies. Yang
\emph{et al.} (2011) reframed patch prioritisation as a problem of
maintaining SLA compliance rather than minimising vulnerability counts,
aligning patch management with the service-level perspective of the
present framework. Kula \emph{et al.} (2019) demonstrated at ING that
managing upstream dependency currency is among the most persistent
challenges in rapid release banking environments, connecting release
velocity to downstream technical debt accumulation in a way directly
measurable from service management data.

\section{Multi-Criteria Decision-Making and
TOPSIS}\label{multi-criteria-decision-making-and-topsis}

\subsection{The Case for Multi-Criteria
Methods}\label{the-case-for-multi-criteria-methods}

Technical debt prioritisation involves the simultaneous weighing of
remediation cost, business risk, security exposure, operational impact,
and regulatory compliance, none of which can be reduced to a single
scalar without information loss (Lenarduzzi \emph{et al.}, 2021).
Single-criterion approaches are demonstrably suboptimal because they
ignore trade-offs that practitioners navigate daily (Alfayez \emph{et
al.}, 2023). MCDM methods provide a mathematically rigorous aggregation
framework for incommensurable criteria. Hwang and Yoon (1981) introduced
TOPSIS, defining the ideal solution as the hypothetical alternative
scoring best on all criteria simultaneously and ranking alternatives by
Euclidean proximity to this ideal. Zanakis \emph{et al.} (1998) found
through simulation that TOPSIS consistently produced rankings among the
most stable and closest to the Pareto-optimal ordering across diverse
problem structures, supporting its use in high-uncertainty
prioritisation environments.

\subsection{TOPSIS: Mechanics, Applications, and Critical
Limitations}\label{topsis-mechanics-applications-and-critical-limitations}

TOPSIS operates by constructing a normalised weighted decision matrix,
identifying ideal best and worst values per criterion, computing each
alternative's Euclidean distance to both reference points, and ranking
by the closeness coefficient, defined as the ratio of the distance to
the ideal worst to the sum of both distances (Albarak \emph{et al.},
2022). The rank reversal problem, formalised by García-Cascales and
Lamata (2012), in which introducing a new alternative changes the
ranking of existing alternatives, is resolved by Yang (no date) through
a modification of the normalisation procedure that defines reference
points independently of the specific alternative set. This correction is
practically significant in enterprise portfolios where services enter
and exit continuously.

Albarak \emph{et al.} (2022) applied TOPSIS combined with portfolio
theory to database normalisation debt, demonstrating that the TOPSIS
ordering identified high-priority targets that a pure effort-based
approach would have indefinitely deferred. Khan and Purohit (2022)
applied a hybrid PROMETHEE-CRITIC and TOPSIS-CRITIC methodology to web
service ranking, showing that objective weighting through CRITIC
combined with TOPSIS produced stable, internally consistent rankings
while resolving rank reversal. Zytoon (2020) applied Delphi-AHP-TOPSIS
to safety inspection prioritisation in a regulated industrial
environment, confirming that TOPSIS generalises to compliance-driven
risk prioritisation with heterogeneous criteria, structurally analogous
to the financial IT context of the present research. Ishak and Wanli
(2020) demonstrated that fuzzy extensions improve robustness when
criterion weights cannot be specified with cardinal precision, relevant
to enterprise settings where expert elicitation is the primary weighting
mechanism.

\subsection{Transferability Critique and Ethical
Dimensions}\label{transferability-critique-and-ethical-dimensions}

These applications provide precedent but not proof of transferability to
the present domain. All existing TOPSIS applications to technical debt
operate on researcher-generated data from controlled development
artefacts. The present research proposes applying TOPSIS to live service
management data extracted from operational records including incident
logs, ITSM change records, and vulnerability scanner outputs, sources
that carry known quality limitations: incident records are
inconsistently classified across teams, change failure rates vary with
recording discipline rather than technical quality alone, and
vulnerability counts depend on scanner coverage rather than actual
exposure. Greco \emph{et al.} (2019) establish that the robustness of
any MCDM-based index is only as strong as its input data quality, and
that sensitivity analysis across weighting schemes is a necessary
validation step. The proposed framework must therefore include a
sensitivity analysis component and explicitly document its data quality
assumptions.

The ethical dimension of criterion weighting in TOPSIS also requires
treatment. Weights assigned to criteria in the decision matrix are not
neutral technical parameters but normative choices about organisational
values. A framework that weights vulnerability debt more heavily than
infrastructure lifecycle debt embeds the judgement that security
exposure matters more than supportability. When used to allocate
remediation budgets in a regulated financial institution, these
weighting choices carry legal and governance implications that cannot be
resolved by mathematical optimisation alone (Greco \emph{et al.}, 2019).
The proposed framework must specify a transparent, documented, and
auditable weight elicitation process involving operations, security,
compliance, and business leadership stakeholders, and must disclose the
resulting weights and their rationale in all governance outputs
produced.

\section{Synthesis: Converging Evidence and the Research
Gap}\label{synthesis-converging-evidence-and-the-research-gap}

\subsection{Cross-Pillar Convergence}\label{cross-pillar-convergence}

Four convergent findings define the theoretical and empirical foundation
for the proposed framework. First, technical debt has expanded
conceptually to encompass every layer of the enterprise IT stack, but
the dominant measurement tools have not followed this expansion,
creating a structural blind spot in the domains most consequential for
financial IT (Kruchten, Nord and Ozkaya, 2012; Yang \emph{et al.}, 2019;
Biazotto \emph{et al.}, 2024). Second, technical debt prioritisation is
empirically and theoretically established as a multi-criteria problem
for which no validated quantitative framework currently exists, a gap
explicitly identified by Lenarduzzi \emph{et al.} (2021). Third, the
operational consequences of unresolved technical debt are measurable and
significant: the 84\% incident reduction documented by de Toledo
\emph{et al.} (2021), the non-linear maintenance cost effects
established by (\textbf{ramasubbuTechnicalDebtReliability2015?}), and
the exploitation timing evidence from Feutrill \emph{et al.} (2018)
collectively confirm that debt translates into quantifiable operational
harm visible in service management records. Fourth, Forsgren, Humble and
Kim (2018) demonstrate that DORA metrics derived from operational data
are valid proxies for technical health when source code is inaccessible,
providing the methodological bridge between the technical debt
literature and the service management data available in financial
trading IT.

\subsection{Cross-Cutting Limitations}\label{cross-cutting-limitations}

Several limitations qualify these conclusions. The empirical base for
financial IT specifically remains thin: Haki \emph{et al.} (2023) is a
single-organisation clinical study with a three-team evaluation, and
Kula \emph{et al.} (2019) addresses release velocity rather than
systematic debt governance. All TOPSIS applications to technical debt
operate on researcher-controlled datasets rather than live operational
records, leaving the data quality challenge unaddressed (Albarak
\emph{et al.}, 2022). The behavioural literature consistently finds that
management intent and stakeholder communication are equal or greater
determinants of remediation outcomes than technical tool quality (Wiese
and Borowa, 2023; Haki \emph{et al.}, 2023), a finding that no
prioritisation algorithm can address but any governance framework must
acknowledge. The ethical dimensions of operational data use, criterion
weighting, and accountability attribution have received no systematic
treatment in the technical debt literature, a gap the present research
explicitly addresses.

\subsection{Statement of the Research
Gap}\label{statement-of-the-research-gap}

No existing framework combines service-level operational metrics,
multi-criteria prioritisation, and financial services domain constraints
into an integrated, auditable technical debt ranking instrument.
Code-centric tools are structurally inapplicable to vendor platform
environments. Single-criterion prioritisation models contradict both the
empirical evidence on practitioner behaviour and the theoretical
requirements of a multi-objective problem. Governance-oriented
qualitative frameworks do not produce the quantitative, repeatable, and
auditable outputs required by risk and compliance functions in regulated
institutions. TOPSIS has not been applied to service-level enterprise
technical debt data, and the financial trading IT domain is absent from
the empirical technical debt literature despite being among the
highest-consequence environments for the risks that unmanaged technical
debt creates.

\subsection{Transition to Research
Questions}\label{transition-to-research-questions}

This gap motivates the two research questions pursued in this
dissertation. The first asks which operational service management
metrics can serve as valid proxies for technical debt severity in
enterprise trading IT systems where source code analysis is not
feasible, and how these metrics can be incorporated into a replicable
and auditable multi-criteria framework. The second asks whether a
TOPSIS-based ranking of these service-level metrics produces
prioritisation outputs more aligned with actual operational risk
exposure than current practitioner heuristics, and whether that
alignment is sufficient to constitute a defensible governance instrument
for a regulated financial institution. Together, these questions
position the proposed framework at the convergence of all four reviewed
pillars and establish the theoretical, empirical, methodological, and
ethical justification for the design science artefact developed in the
subsequent chapters.
