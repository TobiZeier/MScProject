\begin{abstract}

This thesis develops a service-level quantitative framework for
measuring technical debt in mission-critical trading systems. Using a
multi-criteria decision-making approach based on TOPSIS, the framework
evaluates services across five dimensions: code quality, infrastructure,
architecture, documentation, and operations. The model is validated
against empirical data from a Swiss securities trading environment,
demonstrating clear discriminant validity and practical utility for IT
governance.

\section{Background and Motivation}\label{background-and-motivation}

The increasing complexity of mission-critical trading systems has
introduced significant challenges in maintaining software quality over
time. Technical debt --- the implied cost of rework caused by choosing
expedient solutions over more robust approaches --- has become a
critical concern in financial technology environments where system
reliability is paramount.

\section{Problem Statement}\label{problem-statement}

Despite widespread acknowledgement of technical debt as a key risk
factor in enterprise IT, there remains a lack of standardised,
quantitative frameworks for measuring and managing it specifically
within mission-critical trading infrastructures. Current approaches are
largely qualitative and inconsistent, making it difficult to prioritise
remediation efforts effectively.

\section{Research Aims and
Objectives}\label{research-aims-and-objectives}

This thesis aims to develop a service-level quantitative framework for
measuring technical debt in mission-critical trading systems. The
specific objectives are:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  To review existing literature on technical debt measurement frameworks
  and service management practices
\item
  To identify and validate a set of quantifiable technical debt
  indicators relevant to trading systems
\item
  To design and apply a multi-criteria decision-making model (TOPSIS)
  for scoring and prioritising technical debt
\item
  To evaluate the framework against real-world data from a
  mission-critical trading environment
\item
  To assess the practical utility of the framework for IT managers and
  decision-makers
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Questions}\label{research-questions}

The study is guided by the following primary research question:

\begin{quote}
\emph{How can technical debt in mission-critical trading systems be
measured quantitatively at the service level?}
\end{quote}

Secondary questions include:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Which indicators most reliably capture technical debt in trading
  infrastructure?
\item
  How can TOPSIS be applied to rank services by accumulated technical
  debt?
\item
  What thresholds distinguish acceptable from critical debt levels?
\end{itemize}

\section{Significance of the Study}\label{significance-of-the-study}

This research contributes to both academic knowledge and industry
practice by providing a reproducible, data-driven methodology that
bridges IT service management and software quality engineering.

\chapter{Literature Review}\label{literature-review}

\section{Context of the Topic}\label{context-of-the-topic}

Technical debt has become one of the most widely discussed and least
consistently managed challenges in software engineering and enterprise
IT governance (Avgeriou \emph{et al.}, 2024). Originally coined by
Cunningham (1993) as a metaphor for the long-term cost of short-term
technical compromises, the concept has since expanded to encompass
architectural, infrastructure, organisational, and process dimensions of
complex IT systems (Kruchten, Nord and Ozkaya, 2012). Despite growing
academic and practitioner interest, the dominant body of literature
remains anchored at the source code level, relying on static analysis
tools and code-level metrics that are poorly suited to enterprise
environments where operational reliability depends on vendor platforms,
legacy infrastructure, and tightly integrated service stacks rather than
on internally developed software alone (Amanatidis \emph{et al.}, 2020).

This literature review is motivated by a specific and underexplored gap
at the intersection of three fields: technical debt management,
multi-criteria decision-making, and mission-critical enterprise IT
operations. Existing frameworks do not provide a quantitative,
service-level mechanism for measuring and prioritising technical debt in
environments such as trading IT, where the most consequential forms of
debt are observable not in code repositories but in operational outcomes
such as incident frequency, recovery time, patch lag, and change failure
rate (Ramasubbu and Kemerer, 2021). The review therefore examines the
state of knowledge across these three domains and synthesises a
theoretical and methodological foundation for the proposed framework.

\section{Perspective and Framework}\label{perspective-and-framework}

This review adopts a critical, problem-oriented perspective. Rather than
providing an exhaustive catalogue of the technical debt literature, it
is structured to build a coherent argument: that existing measurement
and prioritisation approaches are inadequate for service-level,
enterprise IT contexts, and that a quantitative framework applying
TOPSIS to service management data represents a defensible and novel
contribution. Each thematic section therefore not only summarises
relevant findings but explicitly evaluates the assumptions, limitations,
and gaps in the reviewed work, drawing on contradictory evidence where
available to avoid uncritical acceptance of established positions
(Lenarduzzi \emph{et al.}, 2021).

The review is organised into four thematic areas, each corresponding to
a pillar of the research design:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Conceptual foundations of technical debt} --- origins,
  typologies, and theoretical critiques (Cunningham, 1993; Kruchten,
  Nord and Ozkaya, 2012; Avgeriou \emph{et al.}, 2024)
\item
  \textbf{Existing measurement and prioritisation frameworks} --- tools,
  approaches, and their limitations (Amanatidis \emph{et al.}, 2020;
  Lenarduzzi \emph{et al.}, 2021; Alfayez \emph{et al.}, 2023)
\item
  \textbf{Technical debt in financial and regulated enterprise IT} ---
  domain-specific evidence and contextual constraints (Neil, Häger and
  Andersen, 2009; Forsgren, Humble and Kim, 2018; Ramasubbu and Kemerer,
  2021; Haki \emph{et al.}, 2023)
\item
  \textbf{Multi-criteria decision-making and TOPSIS} --- methodological
  grounding for the prioritisation component (Hwang and Yoon, 1981;
  García-Cascales and Lamata, 2012; Albarak \emph{et al.}, 2022)
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Exclusion Criteria}\label{exclusion-criteria}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Opinion pieces, editorials, and non-empirical practitioner blogs
  without academic grounding
\item

\item
  Duplicate studies superseded by a more comprehensive journal version

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Citation Snowballing}\label{citation-snowballing}

Forward and backward citation tracking was applied to all identified
anchor papers, specifically Cunningham (1993), Kruchten, Nord and Ozkaya

(2018), and Dissanayake \emph{et al.} (2022) to capture foundational

Approximately 140 candidate sources were identified in total; 60+ were
retained as directly relevant following full-text screening against the
criteria above.

\section{Analytical Approach}\label{analytical-approach}

This review employs a narrative synthesis method involving explicit
critical evaluation, as opposed to a formal systematic review protocol.
This approach is justified on two primary grounds.

Firstly, the research encompasses three distinct fields: software
engineering, multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM), and enterprise IT
operations. These disciplines utilise heterogeneous research methods,
terminologies, and quality criteria that are not amenable to unified
meta-analytic treatment. Secondly, the objective of the review is to
construct a coherent theoretical and empirical argument in support of a
novel design science artefact (Hevner \emph{et al.}, 2004). For this
purpose, narrative synthesis represents the methodologically appropriate
approach.

Within this framework, each thematic section applies three analytical
moves consistently:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Synthesis}: identifying convergent findings and established
  consensus positions across reviewed sources
\item
  \textbf{Critique}: surfacing contradictions, limitations, and
  assumptions that qualify or undermine those consensus positions
\item
  \textbf{Positioning}: explicitly relating identified gaps to the
  design decisions of the proposed framework, ensuring full traceability
  between the literature and the research artefact
\end{itemize}

Where quantitative evidence is available, such as the inter-tool
agreement statistics reported by Amanatidis \emph{et al.} (2020), the
84\% incident reduction reported by de Toledo \emph{et al.} (2021), or
the DORA performance benchmarks established by Forsgren, Humble and Kim
(2018), it is used to anchor critical arguments in empirical data rather
than assertion alone. In instances where contradictory findings exist,
for example the divergence between tool-generated TD estimates and
practitioner prioritisation behaviour documented by Alfayez \emph{et
al.} (2023), both positions are represented and their implications for
the proposed framework are discussed explicitly.
