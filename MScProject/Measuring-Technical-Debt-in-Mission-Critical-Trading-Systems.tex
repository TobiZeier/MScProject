\section{Background and Motivation}\label{background-and-motivation}

The increasing complexity of mission-critical trading systems has
introduced significant challenges in maintaining software quality over
time. Technical debt --- the implied cost of rework caused by choosing
expedient solutions over more robust approaches --- has become a
critical concern in financial technology environments where system
reliability is paramount.

\section{Problem Statement}\label{problem-statement}

Despite widespread acknowledgement of technical debt as a key risk
factor in enterprise IT, there remains a lack of standardised,
quantitative frameworks for measuring and managing it specifically
within mission-critical trading infrastructures. Current approaches are
largely qualitative and inconsistent, making it difficult to prioritise
remediation efforts effectively.

\section{Research Aims and
Objectives}\label{research-aims-and-objectives}

This thesis aims to develop a service-level quantitative framework for
measuring technical debt in mission-critical trading systems. The
specific objectives are:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  To review existing literature on technical debt measurement frameworks
  and service management practices
\item
  To identify and validate a set of quantifiable technical debt
  indicators relevant to trading systems
\item
  To design and apply a multi-criteria decision-making model (TOPSIS)
  for scoring and prioritising technical debt
\item
  To evaluate the framework against real-world data from a
  mission-critical trading environment
\item
  To assess the practical utility of the framework for IT managers and
  decision-makers
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Questions}\label{research-questions}

The study is guided by the following primary research question:

\begin{quote}
\emph{How can technical debt in mission-critical trading systems be
measured quantitatively at the service level?}
\end{quote}

Secondary questions include:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Which indicators most reliably capture technical debt in trading
  infrastructure?
\item
  How can TOPSIS be applied to rank services by accumulated technical
  debt?
\item
  What thresholds distinguish acceptable from critical debt levels?
\end{itemize}

\section{Significance of the Study}\label{significance-of-the-study}

This research contributes to both academic knowledge and industry
practice by providing a reproducible, data-driven methodology that
bridges IT service management and software quality engineering.

\chapter{Literature Review}\label{literature-review}

This chapter develops the theoretical and empirical foundation for the
proposed framework. It achieves this objective through a critical review
of four interconnected bodies of literature: the conceptual foundations
of technical debt, existing measurement and prioritisation approaches,
technical debt governance in financial and regulated enterprise IT, and
multi criteria decision-making. Together, these strands support a
synthesis that identifies the research gap and motivates the research
questions addressed in this dissertation.

\section{Context of the Topic}\label{context-of-the-topic}

Technical debt has become both a central concept and a persistent
management problem in software engineering and enterprise IT governance
(Avgeriou \emph{et al.}, 2024). Ward Cunningham first introduced the
metaphor in 1992, describing technical debt as the long term cost of
short term technical compromises (Cunningham, 1992). Over time, the
concept has expanded from a focus on poor code and shortcuts in
implementation to a broader understanding that also includes
architectural, infrastructure, organisational and process aspects of
complex IT systems (Kruchten, Nord and Ozkaya, 2012).

Despite this conceptual expansion, much of the empirical and tool
supported literature remains anchored at the source code level. It
relies heavily on static analysis tools and code based metrics. These
approaches work tolerably well for greenfield development dominated by
in house code. They are much less appropriate for enterprise
environments where operational reliability depends on vendor platforms,
legacy infrastructure, and tightly integrated service stacks rather than
solely on internally developed software (Amanatidis \emph{et al.},
2020).

The present review is motivated by a gap at the intersection of
technical debt management, multi criteria decision-making, and
mission-critical enterprise IT operations. Existing frameworks do not
provide a quantitative, service level mechanism for measuring and
prioritising technical debt in environments such as trading IT. In these
contexts, the most consequential forms of debt manifest not in code
repositories but in operational outcomes such as incident frequency,
recovery time, patch delay, and change failure rate (Ramasubbu and
Kemerer, 2021). The following sections map the state of knowledge across
these three domains and build a theoretical and methodological basis for
the proposed framework.

\section{Perspective and Framework}\label{perspective-and-framework}

The review adopts a critical, problem oriented perspective rather than
an exhaustive cataloguing of all work on technical debt. Its structure
is intended to build a coherent argument: that existing measurement and
prioritisation approaches are not adequate for service level, enterprise
IT contexts, and that a quantitative framework applying TOPSIS to
service management data represents a defensible and novel contribution.

To support this argument, the review is organised into four thematic
areas that align with the four pillars of the research design. The first
examines the conceptual foundations of technical debt. The second
surveys current measurement and prioritisation frameworks and their
limitations. The third focuses on technical debt in financial and
regulated enterprise IT. The fourth introduces multi criteria
decision-making and makes the case for TOPSIS as an appropriate method
in this setting.

a systematic and reproducible approach. It drew on four principal
academic databases: the University of Essex library, Scopus, IEEE Xplore

The selection focused primarily on peer-reviewed journal articles,
conference proceedings and empirical technical reports from the last six
years. Exceptions were made for topics with no recent resarch and for
foundational works that define key concepts or methods, such as
Cunningham (1992) and Hwang and Yoon (1981). All selected sources were
available in full text in English and were directly relevant to at least
one thematic pillar.

Opinion pieces without empirical grounding, studies confined to agile
process improvement without a measurable technical debt component, and
duplicates superseded by more comprehensive journal versions were
excluded. Forward and backward citation snowballing was applied to four
anchor publications: Kruchten, Nord and Ozkaya (2012), Lenarduzzi
\emph{et al.} (2021) and Forsgren, Humble and Kim (2018). The search
yielded approximately 140 candidate sources, of which more than 60 were
retained following full text screening.

\section{Analytical Approach}\label{analytical-approach}

The review uses narrative synthesis rather than a formal systematic
review protocol. Two reasons support this choice. First, the research
spans three disciplines. These include software engineering, systems and
enterprise IT governance, and decision science. These fields differ
significantly in their research methods, terminology and evidence
standards. A single meta analytic framework would not accommodate this
heterogeneity. Second, the aim of the review is to build a coherent
argument in support of a novel design science artefact. In this context,
narrative synthesis is more appropriate than statistical aggregation
(Hevner \emph{et al.}, 2004).

Each thematic section follows the same pattern. It begins by
synthesising convergent findings. It then identifies tensions,
contradictions and gaps. Finally, it positions these gaps in relation to
design decisions for the proposed framework. In this way, the review
remains tightly coupled to the subsequent research design.

\section{Conceptual Foundations of Technical
Debt}\label{conceptual-foundations-of-technical-debt}

The first thematic area traces the evolution of the technical debt
concept. It moves from the original metaphor to later theoretical
developments and then to the expansion of the concept beyond software
into systems engineering, infrastructure and vendor platforms.
Understanding this evolution is essential because most existing tools
and practices are still tuned to an earlier, narrower view of technical
debt and so misalign with the realities of contemporary enterprise IT.

\subsection{Origins and the Metaphor}\label{origins-and-the-metaphor}

Cunningham (1992) introduced the metaphor of technical debt to explain
his refactoring strategy to non-technical stakeholders. He compared
shipping ``not quite right'' code to taking on financial debt. The
development team gains speed and delivers earlier, but every additional
maintenance effort on that code represents interest. If the principal is
not repaid through refactoring, the cost of working with the system
grows until further change becomes very difficult or impossible.

Several influential thinkers refined the concept. McConnell (2008)
distinguished between unintentional debt, arising from poor practice,
and intentional debt, incurred as a conscious trade-off, and introduced
a cost structure of principal, recurring interest, and compounding
interest. Fowler (2009) added a quadrant distinguishing reckless from
prudent debt and deliberate from inadvertent debt, arguing that even the
best teams inevitably accumulate some inadvertent debt as their
understanding of the problem matures. Gat (2010) cautioned that when
debt accumulates to the point where it equals or exceeds the value of
the system itself, the code becomes ``toxic'' and further investment
yields negative returns. Kruchten, Nord and Ozkaya (2012) later
formalised these ideas into a taxonomy covering code, design,
architectural, test, build and documentation debt.

\subsection{Theoretical Development}\label{theoretical-development}

Subsequent empirical studies have confirmed and extended this view.
Besker (2020) reported that developers in industrial settings spend
roughly 23\% of their time dealing with the consequences of existing
technical debt, with architectural debt exerting the strongest negative
influence on productivity. Bedi and Kaur (2022) used regression analysis
on open source repositories and found that commit frequency, lines of
code, code smells, test coverage are all significant predictors of
technical debt, whereas developer reputation metrics are not. Their work
suggests that technical debt is driven primarily by development
practices rather than by the perceived quality of individual developers.

Ahmad \emph{et al.} (2026) approached the topic qualitatively,
interviewing experts from four international companies. They identified
five non-technical dimensions of debt accumulation in large scale agile
environments: social dynamics, process, people, documentation and
requirements. Across these dimensions, a lack of communication and
collaboration emerged as the dominant driver. This reinforces the view
of technical debt as a sociotechnical phenomenon.

Vidoni, Codabux and Fard (2022) advanced a more radical theoretical
argument through a game theoretic lens. They argued that in complex
systems technical debt cannot be completely removed and that its effects
cannot be considered fully controlled, even when individual items are
repaid. The concept of ``infinite technical debt'' challenges the
assumption, implicit in many repayment focused frameworks, that debt is
always recoverable given sufficient effort. It also raises an ethical
concern: if a system has passed into a state where meaningful repayment
is no longer feasible, continuing to emphasise local repayments may
offer an illusion of governance while delaying necessary replacement
decisions.

\subsection{Expanding the Conceptual
Perimeter}\label{expanding-the-conceptual-perimeter}

The conceptual perimeter of technical debt has progressively widened to
include broader systems and lifecycle concerns. Kleinwaks, Batchelor and
Bradley (2023) conducted a systematic review of technical debt in
systems engineering and found that requirements, architecture,
integration artefacts and verification artefacts can all accumulate
debt. They also concluded that many software oriented technical debt
approaches are poorly adapted to multidisciplinary systems engineering
lifecycles. Hanssen, Brataas and Martini (2019) identified scalability
debt as a distinct subtype of technical debt triggered by regulatory
change. In an open banking case study they showed that architectural
decisions that were sound before the introduction of the EU Payment
Services Directive 2 (PSD2) became sources of debt once new scalability
requirements were imposed without revisiting the architecture. This
illustrates how external regulatory change can convert previously
adequate designs into liabilities. Yang \emph{et al.} (2019) proposed a
taxonomy of Commercial Off-the-Shelf (COTS) related technical debt that
covers seven key types of debt arising during system acquisition and
integration. These include vendor platform obsolescence and integration
constraints. Managing such forms of debt requires different tools from
those used for internally developed code. For financial trading IT,
where vendor platforms and COTS products are central, this broader view
is particularly relevant. Many of the most consequential debts in this
domain---platform debt, infrastructure debt and vulnerability debt---are
not detectable by standard static analysis.

\section{Measurement and Prioritisation
Frameworks}\label{measurement-and-prioritisation-frameworks}

The second thematic area examines how technical debt is measured and
prioritised in practice. It argues that current measurement is heavily
concentrated at the code level and that existing prioritisation
frameworks lack the multidimensional and quantitative rigour required
for robust governance in enterprise settings.

\subsection{The Dominance of Static Analysis and Its
Limits}\label{the-dominance-of-static-analysis-and-its-limits}

Static code analysis tools such as SonarQube, SQALE and CAST dominate
current practice. They typically operationalise technical debt as a
weighted sum of estimated remediation effort. The estimates are derived
from rule violations detected by automated scanners (Biazotto \emph{et
al.}, 2024). In a systematic mapping study, Biazotto \emph{et al.}
(2024) identified 178 primary studies and 121 automation artefacts for
technical debt management. They found that most artefacts operate at the
code level, that many still require substantial human intervention even
at higher automation levels, and that end to end automation across the
technical debt management lifecycle is rare. The focus on code leaves
infrastructure level and platform level debt largely unaddressed.
Ampatzoglou \emph{et al.} (2022) presented SDK4ED as a comprehensive
industry oriented platform integrating several code level techniques. A
three-year evaluation suggested that SDK4ED can deliver financial
benefits to software organisations. At the same time, the platform
offers only limited support for debt related to infrastructure or vendor
platforms. Tornhill and Borg (2022) analysed 39 proprietary production
codebases drawn from multiple industries. They found that poor code
quality is statistically associated with higher defect density, longer
remediation cycles and reduced developer throughput. This confirms that
code level debt has clear business consequences. It does not, however,
change the fact that an exclusive focus on code creates blind spots.
Organisations may allocate significant resources to ``safe'' refactoring
while leaving critical legacy infrastructure and unsupported components
unaddressed. Amanatidis \emph{et al.} (2020) evaluated the agreement
between different technical debt assessment tools applied to 50 open
source projects. They observed substantial divergence: tools that assess
the same codebase produce different debt estimates and highlight
different violation sets. No single tool consistently dominated. This
implies that a debt figure generated by one tool is not an objective
measurement but a function of that tool's ruleset and thresholds.
Albuquerque \emph{et al.} (2023) reviewed 150 primary studies on
intelligent techniques for technical debt management. They found that
most approaches remain at low to intermediate levels of automation and
that human involvement is still required at several stages. They also
noted that infrastructure debt and process debt remain significantly
understudied compared to code and architectural debt.

\subsection{The Prioritisation Gap}\label{the-prioritisation-gap}

Lenarduzzi \emph{et al.} (2021) carried out the most comprehensive
review of technical debt prioritisation strategies to date. They
analysed 44 primary studies and found that existing approaches cluster
around cost based, value based and risk based strategies. Most of these
approaches adopt a single primary criterion. Quantitative methods are
less common than qualitative or heuristic techniques, and empirical
validation on industrial datasets is rare. The authors concluded that
the field lacks agreement on which factors matter, how to measure them
and what constitutes an adequate prioritisation process. This conclusion
directly motivates the quantitative prioritisation contribution of the
present work. Concrete evidence demonstrates the cost of inadequate
prioritisation. de Toledo \emph{et al.} (2021) showed in a production
microservices system that targeted repayment of architectural technical
debt items led to an 84\% reduction in incidents. This result anchors
the abstract case for technical debt management in a measurable
operational outcome and suggests incident reduction as a meaningful
evaluation criterion for prioritisation frameworks.

Empirical studies also reveal a clear gap between tool generated
priorities and practitioner behaviour. Alfayez \emph{et al.} (2023)
studied 89 practitioners from four companies. They found that most
practitioners considered both value and cost when deciding which items
to repay, rather than following a single strategy. They also reported
that perceived value was highly context dependent and subjective, which
challenges the assumption that any single ranking rule can capture all
relevant considerations. Lim, Taksande and Seaman (2012) interviewed
practitioners across several organisations. They found that repayment
decisions are strongly shaped by organisational pressures, interpersonal
dynamics and competing business priorities. These factors are difficult
to encode in algorithms. The implication is that quantitative
prioritisation tools should be seen as decision support rather than
decision replacement. From a managerial perspective, Wiese and Borowa
(2023) interviewed 16 IT managers and found, that most did not have a
formal technical debt management process in place. The primary challenge
they identified was communicating about technical debt across technical
and business stakeholders. Stochel \emph{et al.} (2023) proposed the
Continuous Debt Valuation Approach (CoDVA), which assigns a financial
value to each debt item based on remediation cost, interest rate and
business risk exposure. Case studies show that this valuation can
significantly change repayment priorities compared with effort only
approaches. However, CoDVA still relies on development level data and
does not incorporate signals from operational service management, such
as incident rates or change failure rates.

These studies also highlight an ethical dimension. Any prioritisation
framework embeds value judgements about which systems, users and risks
matter most. In financial institutions, priority scores may influence
funding, staffing and post incident accountability. Where weighting
schemes and decision rules are opaque, they may conceal these value
choices under a veneer of technical objectivity.

\section{Technical Debt in Financial and Regulated Enterprise
IT}\label{technical-debt-in-financial-and-regulated-enterprise-it}

The third thematic area applies the general insights from the technical
debt literature to the specific setting of financial services and
regulated trading IT. It focuses on how debt accumulates in these
environments, how it translates into operational harm, and how related
work in security and patch management quantifies this harm.

\subsection{The Financial Services
Context}\label{the-financial-services-context}

Financial services organisations are characterised by complex, layered
legacies, strict regulatory compliance requirements and stringent
service availability obligations. Architectures often depend on vendor
platforms and COTS products that cannot be freely refactored (Haki
\emph{et al.}, 2023).

Haki \emph{et al.} (2023) conducted clinical research at Credit Suisse,
a former global financial services institution based in Zurich. They
designed and introduced a digital nudge for technical debt management
across more than 3,000 IT applications. As part of this work, they
defined a technical debt taxonomy with four main categories: code
quality debt, infrastructure lifecycle debt, vulnerability debt and
automation debt. Infrastructure lifecycle debt was measured as
cumulative months of unsupported components. Vulnerability debt was
derived from weighted counts of vulnerabilities detected by network
scanners. Automation debt reflected build and deployment maturity. This
taxonomy is important because it shows that, at scale, a major financial
institution assigns at least as much importance to infrastructure and
vulnerability debt as to code level debt.

The empirical basis of the evaluation is, however, limited. The
quantitative analysis focused on three development teams. Two teams
reduced their debt index over one year and one did not. The authors
identified management intent as the decisive factor, rather than the
design of the nudge itself. Furthermore, the use of a clinical research
design, with researchers embedded in the organisation, raises questions
about independence. These factors do not invalidate the conceptual
contribution, but they suggest caution in generalising from the observed
outcomes.

Rolland and Lyytinen investigated the organisational dynamics of
architectural debt accumulation in a large Scandinavian financial
institution they called BankAlpha (Rolland and Lyytinen, 2021). They
conducted eleven in-depth interviews with business managers, enterprise
architects, IT architects and software developers, supplemented by
meeting notes and archival documents. Their analysis applied Carlile's
knowledge boundary framework to explain why architectural debt becomes
invisible across distributed agile teams. They identified two persistent
tensions. The first is between decentralised feature delivery teams,
which optimise for local throughput, and the architectural governance
required to manage debt at the system level. The second is between the
pace of digital innovation and the long-term sustainability of the
architecture that must support it. Both tensions are intensified in
financial services by compliance obligations and competitive pressure to
release new products rapidly. The study is notable for grounding these
dynamics in empirical data from a regulated banking institution. Its
primary limitation is the single-case design. Whether the patterns
observed at BankAlpha generalise to other institutions remains an open
question. Nevertheless, the paper offers a theoretically grounded
account of why architectural debt accumulates even in organisations that
acknowledge its importance, and why governance instruments that rely
solely on technical measurement are unlikely to resolve the underlying
organisational conflicts.

\subsection{Enterprise Systems and the Outsourcing
Context}\label{enterprise-systems-and-the-outsourcing-context}

Banker, Liang and Ramasubbu (2020) examined 26 firms operating a
commercial COTS CRM system over its full eleven-year lifecycle. Using
propensity score matching against firms with zero technical debt, they
found that a 10\% increase in technical debt reduced gross return on
assets by 16\% on average. Critically, this penalty more than doubled
within six years of adoption as code decay compounded structural
complexity. While all firms initially benefited from system
customisation, those carrying median debt levels saw the net value of
their system turn negative by year five, a pattern robust to alternative
performance measures including asset turnover, gross margin, and
operating income.

In a later study, they extended this analysis to 1,824 outsourced
remediation projects involving 424 client organisations (Ramasubbu and
Kemerer, 2021). They found that ``control balancing'' between client and
vendor improved remediation outcomes only when both parties had agreed a
long term migration roadmap. In the absence of such a roadmap, increased
coordination actually harmed performance. For prioritisation frameworks,
this implies that ranking debt items without embedding them in a
strategic roadmap can be counterproductive.

The study ``Getting Trapped in Technical Debt'' provides a complementary
perspective (Rinta-Kahila \emph{et al.}, 2023). In an explanatory case
study of a global manufacturing firm's COTS implementation, the authors
showed how staged legacy replacement, combined with recurrent
customisations to restore local flexibility, led to self reinforcing
architectural debt accumulation. Over time, the system moved into a debt
constrained state in which further modifications became overly
difficult. A system dynamics model captured reinforcing feedback loops
between customisation decisions, debt accumulation and declining digital
options. This work suggests that organisations often do not consciously
choose to neglect technical debt. Instead, they are trapped by
interactions between technical design choices and organisational
routines. Standard code level tools are not capable of revealing such
dynamics. Governance level visibility of infrastructure and
architectural debt is required to detect and address them before they
become irreversible.

\subsection{DORA Metrics as Operational Debt
Proxies}\label{dora-metrics-as-operational-debt-proxies}

Forsgren, Humble and Kim (2018) developed and validated the DORA metrics
across more than 2,000 organisations. They showed that four indicators,
deployment frequency, lead time for changes, mean time to restore
service, and change failure rate, distinguish high performing from low
performing delivery teams. A crucial feature of these metrics is that
they can be derived from operational systems such as CI/CD pipelines and
IT service management tools without access to source code.

In contrast, Paudel \emph{et al.} (2025) investigated the relationship
between technical debt density and lead time across six components at a
large fintech company. The results were mixed. Two components showed a
moderate positive correlation, two showed a moderate negative
correlation, and two showed no meaningful relationship. Technical debt
density explained between five and forty-one percent of lead time
variance depending on the component. The authors concluded that
technical debt alone cannot explain lead time and that other variables,
including change size, component ownership and team structure, are
likely confounding factors. This result tempers claims about the
operational impact of code-level debt and reinforces the case for
multi-criteria measurement rather than reliance on a single proxy.

Using operational service management data as a basis for technical debt
prioritisation raises important ethical questions regardless of the
specific metric used. Such records are generated and owned by operations
teams. Development teams may not have consented to their use in
performance evaluations. A framework that ranks services based on
operational indicators may influence budgets, staffing and reputational
assessments. Any use of such data therefore requires explicit data
governance principles and transparency about how metrics are attributed,
aggregated and interpreted.

\subsection{Security Debt and Patch
Management}\label{security-debt-and-patch-management}

Security related technical debt is most visible in the literature on
patch management and vulnerability handling. Tizio, Armellini and
Massacci (2023) analysed a dataset of 86 Advanced Persistent Threats
(APT) and 350 campaigns dated between 2008 and 2020 to quantify the risk
of different software update strategies. Contrary to common assumption,
most APT campaigns exploited publicly known vulnerabilities rather than
zero-days. Enterprises delaying updates by one month faced 4.9 times
higher odds of compromise compared to immediate patching, rising to 9.1
times at three months.

Markkanen and Frantti (2023) reviewed the academic literature and
current NIST guidance on patch management planning to identify gaps and
propose enriched recommendations for enterprise organisations. They
argue that uniform, time-based patching policies are increasingly
inadequate given the shrinking window between vulnerability disclosure
and exploitation, and that organisations should instead treat patching
as a risk-based, organisation-wide business priority rather than a
purely technical responsibility. Nurse (2025) framed patch
prioritisation as a problem of maintaining service-level agreement (SLA)
compliance, noting that business partners and clients drive patching
decisions through supply chain agreements and contractual requirements.
This aligns patch management with the service level focus of the present
research.

Kula \emph{et al.} (2019) provided further evidence from ING, where they
studied release practices and dependency management. They showed that
keeping upstream dependencies current is a persistent challenge in rapid
release banking environments and that delays in dependency updates
contribute to technical debt. This finding is consistent with the
broader argument, supported by Forsgren, Humble and Kim as well as
Paudel \emph{et al.}, that technical debt manifests in ways that are
detectable through operational service management data (Forsgren, Humble
and Kim, 2018; Paudel \emph{et al.}, 2025)

\section{Multi-Criteria Decision-Making and
TOPSIS}\label{multi-criteria-decision-making-and-topsis}

The fourth thematic area turns to decision science. The previous
sections established that technical debt in enterprise IT is
multidimensional, that current tools largely ignore the most important
dimensions in enterprise settings, and that no validated quantitative
prioritisation framework exists. This section argues that multi-criteria
decision-making (MCDM) methods are well suited to the prioritisation
problem and that TOPSIS, in particular, is an appropriate method for the
proposed framework.

\subsection{The Case for Multi-Criteria
Methods}\label{the-case-for-multi-criteria-methods}

Technical debt prioritisation involves balancing several criteria at
once. These include remediation cost, business value, security risk,
operational impact and regulatory compliance. Existing practice and
empirical studies show that practitioners weigh these dimensions
together rather than focusing on a single factor (Alfayez \emph{et al.},
2023). Collapsing them into a single ad-hoc indicator risks discarding
information that matters in real decisions. Lenarduzzi \emph{et al.}
(2021) concluded that the absence of a validated, quantitative, multi
criteria framework is a central obstacle to evidence based technical
debt governance. MCDM methods provide formal mechanisms for integrating
multiple, incommensurable criteria. Among these methods, TOPSIS is
attractive because of its intuitive interpretation and computational
simplicity.

Hwang and Yoon (1981) introduced TOPSIS, defining the ideal solution as
the hypothetical alternative scoring best on all criteria simultaneously
and ranking alternatives by Euclidean proximity to this ideal. Zanakis
\emph{et al.} (1998) compared several MCDM methods and found that TOPSIS
tends to produce rankings that are stable and close to the Pareto
optimal ordering across a range of problem structures. This supports its
use in prioritisation tasks under uncertainty.

\subsection{TOPSIS: Mechanics, Applications, and Critical
Limitations}\label{topsis-mechanics-applications-and-critical-limitations}

TOPSIS operates by constructing a normalised weighted decision matrix,
identifying ideal best and worst values per criterion, computing each
alternative's Euclidean distance to both reference points, and ranking
by the closeness coefficient, defined as the ratio of the distance to
the ideal worst to the sum of both distances (Albarak \emph{et al.},
2022). A well known issue with TOPSIS is rank reversal: the addition or
removal of alternatives can change the ranking of existing options
(García-Cascales and Lamata, 2012). Yang (2020) proposed an adjustment
to the normalisation process to mitigate rank reversal by defining
reference points independently of the specific set of alternatives. This
is particularly relevant for enterprise portfolios where services are
regularly added, modified or decommissioned.

Shekhovtsov and Sałabun (2020) compared TOPSIS and VIKOR across several
decision problems. They found that the two methods produce similar
rankings when criterion weights are well specified, but diverge when
weights are uncertain or criteria conflict strongly. They suggested that
TOPSIS is preferable when the objective is a global ranking of
alternatives across all criteria, whereas VIKOR is better suited to
identifying compromise solutions between conflicting criteria. Because
the present research aims to generate a ranked list of services for
remediation, rather than to identify compromise solutions, TOPSIS
appears more appropriate.

Several studies demonstrate that TOPSIS can be applied in technical and
risk intensive contexts. Albarak \emph{et al.} (2022) and colleagues
combined TOPSIS with portfolio theory to prioritise database
normalisation debt. Their results showed that TOPSIS highlighted high
priority debt items that would likely have been deferred under effort
only approaches. Khan and Purohit (2022) applied a hybrid PROMETHEE
CRITIC and TOPSIS CRITIC approach to web service ranking, using CRITIC
to derive objective weights. Zytoon (2020) used a Delphi--AHP--TOPSIS
combination to prioritise safety inspections in a regulated industrial
environment. Ishak and Wanli (2020) applied fuzzy AHP--TOPSIS to improve
robustness when weights cannot be specified with precision. All of these
studies provide precedents for using TOPSIS to support multi criteria
decisions under risk and regulation.

\section{Transferability Critique and Ethical
Dimensions}\label{transferability-critique-and-ethical-dimensions}

Although these applications offer relevant precedents, none of them
operates on live service management data in large enterprise settings.
Most rely on researcher defined scenarios, synthetic datasets or
controlled development artefacts. The proposed research differs by
applying TOPSIS to operational records such as incident logs, ITSM
change data and vulnerability scan outputs. These data sources are known
to have quality issues. Incident classifications can vary between teams.
Recorded change failure rates can be influenced by documentation
practices as much as by technical quality. Vulnerability counts depend
on scanner coverage and configuration.

Greco \emph{et al.} (2019) examined composite indices based on MCDM
methods and emphasised that their robustness depends strongly on input
data quality. They argued that sensitivity analysis with respect to
criterion weights is a necessary component of any such index. For the
proposed framework, this implies that data limitations must be
explicitly acknowledged, that the sensitivity of rankings to weight
changes must be studied, and that outputs must be interpreted with
appropriate caution.

The ethical dimension of criterion weighting is equally important.
Weights are not neutral technical parameters but normative choices about
organisational priorities. A framework that places a high weight on
vulnerability debt relative to infrastructure lifecycle debt expresses a
judgement that security exposure is more important than supportability.
In a regulated financial institution, such choices have governance and
possibly legal implications (Greco \emph{et al.}, 2019). The framework
must therefore include a transparent and auditable process for weight
elicitation. This process should consider aspects from operations,
security, risk and business leadership perspectives, and the resulting
weights and their rationale should be documented and visible in all
governance outputs.

\section{Synthesis: Converging Evidence and the Research
Gap}\label{synthesis-converging-evidence-and-the-research-gap}

The four thematic sections together define the conceptual,
methodological, contextual and technical landscape within which this
research is situated. This synthesis draws out the main points of
convergence across the literature, acknowledges important limitations
and articulates the research gap that the proposed framework addresses.

\subsection{Cross Pillar Convergence}\label{cross-pillar-convergence}

Four converging findings are particularly significant. First, the
concept of technical debt has expanded to encompass all layers of the
enterprise IT stack, but measurement practice has not kept pace. The
dominant tools still focus on code level debt, leaving infrastructure
lifecycle debt, platform obsolescence and other critical forms of
enterprise debt largely unmeasured (Kruchten, Nord and Ozkaya, 2012;
Yang \emph{et al.}, 2019; Biazotto \emph{et al.}, 2024).

Second, technical debt prioritisation is both empirically and
theoretically a multi criteria problem. No widely accepted quantitative
framework currently exists that can integrate cost, value, risk and
operational impact into a single, auditable ranking (Lenarduzzi \emph{et
al.}, 2021). Practitioner studies confirm that both developers and
managers routinely consider multiple factors when making repayment
decisions (Pina, Seaman and Goldman, 2022; Alfayez \emph{et al.}, 2023).

Third, the consequences of unmanaged technical debt are both measurable
and substantial. Targeted repayment of architectural debt can
dramatically reduce incidents (de Toledo \emph{et al.}, 2021). The
financial harm of technical debt compounds progressively over a system's
lifecycle (Banker, Liang and Ramasubbu, 2020). Repeated customisation of
COTS systems can drive organisations into debt-constrained states
(Rinta-Kahila \emph{et al.}, 2023). Poor code quality reduces throughput
and increases defects (Tornhill and Borg, 2022). Delayed patching
increases vulnerability to exploitation (Feutrill \emph{et al.}, 2018).
Together these studies show that technical debt produces quantifiable,
progressive operational harm that is visible in service level data
before it becomes evident in code.

Fourth, DORA metrics and related operational indicators offer a viable
bridge between technical debt and observable service level performance.
Forsgren, Humble and Kim (2018) showed that these metrics capture
aspects of technical health even when source code is inaccessible.
Paudel \emph{et al.} (2025) provided further evidence by linking
technical debt accumulation directly to degradation in lead time. This
suggests that service management data can serve as a legitimate
measurement basis for technical debt in vendor dominated trading IT
environments.

\subsection{Cross Cutting Limitations}\label{cross-cutting-limitations}

At the same time, several limitations qualify these conclusions. The
empirical base for financial IT and trading systems remains relatively
thin. Studies such as Haki \emph{et al.'s} work at Credit Suisse and
\emph{Kula et al.'s} analysis at ING provide valuable insights but are
restricted to single organisations (Kula \emph{et al.}, 2019; Haki
\emph{et al.}, 2023).

Existing applications of TOPSIS and other MCDM methods to technical debt
rely on controlled datasets rather than live operational records
(Albarak \emph{et al.}, 2022; Khan and Purohit, 2022). The comparative
work of (\textbf{salabunComparativeCaseStudy2020?}) shows that method
choice matters less than weight choice when weights are uncertain, which
underscores the importance of careful and transparent weight
determination.

Behavioural studies emphasise that management intent, incentives and
communication patterns often exert a stronger influence on remediation
outcomes than technical tools do (Besker, 2020; Wiese and Borowa, 2023;
Haki \emph{et al.}, 2023). Technical frameworks can support, but not
replace, governance and leadership.

Finally, the ethical dimensions of operating on service level
operational data, assigning criterion weights and attributing
responsibility for technical debt have received little explicit
attention in the technical debt literature. The present work responds to
this gap by embedding transparency and governance considerations into
the design and evaluation of the proposed framework.

\subsection{Statement of the Research
Gap}\label{statement-of-the-research-gap}

No existing framework integrates service level operational metrics,
multi criteria prioritisation and the specific constraints of financial
trading IT into a single, auditable technical debt ranking instrument.
Code centric tools are structurally inapplicable to many vendor hosted
and regulated platforms. Single criterion models conflict with both the
empirical evidence on practitioner behaviour and the theoretical
structure of the prioritisation problem. Qualitative governance
frameworks lack the quantitative precision and repeatability required by
risk and compliance functions in regulated institutions.

Moreover, TOPSIS has not been applied to service level technical debt
data in an enterprise setting. The financial trading IT domain is almost
absent from empirical technical debt research, despite being one of the
environments where the risks associated with unmanaged technical debt
are most severe.

\subsection{Transition to Research
Questions}\label{transition-to-research-questions}

These observations motivate two research questions. The first concerns
measurement: which operational service management metrics can act as
valid proxies for technical debt severity in enterprise trading IT
systems where source code analysis is not feasible, and how can these
metrics be combined into a replicable and auditable multi criteria
framework? The second concerns evaluation: does a TOPSIS based ranking
of services, constructed from such metrics, align more closely with
actual operational risk exposure than existing practitioner heuristics,
and is that alignment sufficient for the framework to function as a
defensible governance instrument in a regulated financial institution?

Taken together, these questions position the proposed framework at the
convergence of the four reviewed pillars and set the stage for the
design science work presented in the subsequent chapters.
